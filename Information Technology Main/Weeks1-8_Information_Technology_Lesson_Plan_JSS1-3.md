DETAILED LESSON PLAN – JSS 1
-----------------------------

- **Date:** Monday, 15th September 2025
- **Class:** JSS 1
- **Duration:** 45 minutes
- **Period:** First
- **Topic:** Historical Development of Computers
- **Subtopic:** Early Counting Devices
- **Reference Materials:** NERDC Basic Science and Technology for JSS 1; NERDC Information Technology for Junior Secondary Schools; Internet resources on early computing devices
- **Instructional Materials:** Pictures of counting stones, tally sticks, abacus, Napier's bones; actual abacus; timeline chart
- **Entry Behaviour:** Students have used fingers and sticks to count small numbers.
- **Behavioural Objectives:** By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
  1. Describe early methods people used for counting.
  2. Identify at least three early counting devices and their uses.
  3. Arrange early counting devices in chronological order.
  4. Demonstrate how to use the abacus for simple addition.
  5. Explain why the need for accurate counting led to the development of better devices.

### Lesson Presentation

**Step 1 – Introduction:** Teacher asks students to share how they count objects at home (fingers, stones, sticks) and writes responses on the board.

**Step 2 – Presentation:** Teacher introduces early counting devices such as tally sticks, counting stones, cowrie shells, and the abacus.

**Step 3 – Explanation:** Teacher describes each device, its origin, and how it was used for counting and record keeping, using a timeline chart.

**Step 4 – Application:** Students handle an abacus or improvised counting frame to practise counting and simple addition in groups.

**Step 5 – Evaluation:** Teacher asks students to name early counting devices, arrange them in order, and demonstrate counting with the abacus.

### Content Summary

1. People first used **fingers, stones, and sticks** to count and keep records.
2. Early counting devices include **tally sticks**, **cowrie shells**, **pebbles**, **abacus**, and **Napier's bones**.
3. These devices helped traders, farmers, and tax collectors to count goods and record numbers accurately.
4. The search for faster and more accurate counting led to the invention of mechanical and electronic computers.

### Conclusion

Teacher emphasises that modern computers developed from simple counting tools created to solve everyday problems.

### Student Lesson Notes

- Early humans counted with **fingers, stones, and sticks** before making special tools.
- **Tally sticks** were marked pieces of wood used to keep scores or records.
- The **abacus** is a frame with beads used for counting and performing simple arithmetic.
- **Napier's bones** were rods with multiplication tables that made calculation faster.
- These early devices were the foundation for the computers we use today.

DETAILED LESSON PLAN – JSS 2
-----------------------------

- **Date:** Wednesday, 17th September 2025
- **Class:** JSS 2
- **Duration:** 45 minutes
- **Period:** Second
- **Topic:** Computer Software
- **Subtopic:** Introduction and Definition of Software
- **Reference Materials:** NERDC Basic Science and Technology for JSS 2; NERDC Information Technology for Junior Secondary Schools; Computer Studies Textbook for JSS 2
- **Instructional Materials:** Computer system, projector slides showing software types, flash cards with software names
- **Entry Behaviour:** Students have used school computers for typing and educational games.
- **Behavioural Objectives:** By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
  1. Define software correctly.
  2. Differentiate between hardware and software with examples.
  3. Identify system software and application software.
  4. Mention examples of common software used in school and at home.
  5. Explain the importance of software in operating the computer.

### Lesson Presentation

**Step 1 – Introduction:** Teacher shows a computer system and asks students to name the physical parts they can see and the programs they use on it.

**Step 2 – Presentation:** Teacher defines software as the set of instructions or programs that tell the hardware what to do.

**Step 3 – Explanation:** Teacher distinguishes between system software (operating system, utility programs) and application software (word processors, games, browsers) using examples.

**Step 4 – Application:** Students work in groups to sort software names on flash cards into system software and application software, explaining their choices.

**Step 5 – Evaluation:** Teacher asks oral questions on the definition of software, differences between hardware and software, and examples of each type of software.

### Content Summary

1. **Software** is a collection of programs or instructions that tell the computer how to perform tasks.
2. Computer system is made up of **hardware** (physical parts) and **software** (programs and data).
3. **System software** controls the hardware and includes the operating system (Windows, Linux), utilities (antivirus, backup tools), and language translators.
4. **Application software** helps users perform specific tasks like writing (Microsoft Word), drawing (Paint), browsing (Chrome), or learning (educational apps).

### Conclusion

Teacher restates that computers cannot work without software and encourages students to note the software they use daily.

### Student Lesson Notes

- **Software** is the invisible part of the computer that tells the hardware what to do.
- Two main types of software are **system software** and **application software**.
- System software includes **operating systems** (Windows, Linux, Android) and **utility programs** (antivirus, disk cleanup).
- Application software includes **word processors**, **spreadsheets**, **games**, **web browsers**, and **media players**.
- Hardware is the body of the computer, while software is the mind that gives instructions.

- **Behavioural Objectives:** By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
  1. Describe how early counting devices were used to solve basic counting problems.
  2. Explain difficulties faced when counting large numbers with devices like fingers, stones, and the abacus.
  3. Give examples of situations where early devices could not keep accurate records.
  4. Demonstrate how errors occur when using simple devices for big numbers.
  5. Discuss why the limitations of early devices led to the development of modern computers.

### Lesson Presentation

**Step 1 – Introduction:** Teacher asks students to count a large pile of bottle tops with their fingers and note the time taken and errors observed.

**Step 2 – Presentation:** Teacher reviews early devices such as tally sticks, pebbles, and abacus, highlighting how they were used for trade and tax collection.

**Step 3 – Explanation:** Teacher explains the challenges of using these devices for large numbers: time-consuming, easy to misplace items, limited memory, and difficulty performing complex calculations.

**Step 4 – Application:** Students work in groups to attempt counting tasks using pebbles and an abacus; they record difficulties encountered and share findings.

**Step 5 – Evaluation:** Teacher asks students to describe two problems faced when using early devices for large numbers and suggest why better machines were needed.

### Content Summary

1. Early counting tools such as **fingers**, **stones**, **tally sticks**, and **abacus** helped people keep simple records.
2. Counting large numbers with these devices caused problems like **loss of counters**, **slow calculations**, and **errors in recording**.
3. Traders and tax collectors needed faster, more accurate methods, leading to the invention of mechanical and electronic calculators.
4. The desire to solve counting problems encouraged the development of modern computers.

### Conclusion

Teacher summarises the limitations of early counting devices and links them to the emergence of modern computing systems.

### Student Lesson Notes

- Early counting devices worked for small numbers but became **slow and confusing** when numbers were large.
- People could easily **lose stones or tally marks**, making records incorrect.
- The **abacus** was faster but still required skilled users and could not store information for long periods.
- Because of these problems, inventors created better machines that could count and calculate accurately.

- **Instructional Materials:** Pictures and models of the Jacquard loom, Herman Hollerith’s punched card machine, differential analyzer; timeline chart; short documentary clip

  1. Identify at least three electro-mechanical counting devices.
  2. Describe how electricity and mechanical parts worked together in these devices.
  3. State the contributions of each device to modern computing.
  4. Arrange electro-mechanical devices in chronological order on a class timeline.
  5. Compare electro-mechanical devices with earlier mechanical devices.

### Lesson Presentation

**Step 1 – Introduction:** Teacher displays a timeline with blank spaces for electro-mechanical devices and asks students to recall earlier mechanical inventions.

**Step 2 – Presentation:** Teacher explains that electro-mechanical devices combined electrical power with mechanical parts to improve speed and accuracy.

**Step 3 – Explanation:** Teacher describes the **Jacquard loom** (1801) using punched cards to control weaving patterns, **Hollerith’s punched card tabulator** (1890) for census data, and the **differential analyzer** (1930s) for complex calculations.

**Step 4 – Application:** Students rotate through stations examining device pictures and noting key features, then place device cards correctly on the timeline.

**Step 5 – Evaluation:** Teacher asks students to name devices, explain how they worked, and state their impact on modern computing.

### Content Summary

1. Electro-mechanical devices used **electricity** and **mechanical parts** to process information faster than purely mechanical tools.
2. The **Jacquard loom** used punched cards to automate weaving, showing how instructions can control machines.
3. **Hollerith’s tabulating machine** read punched cards to process the U.S. census quickly.
4. The **differential analyzer** used motors and gears to solve complex mathematical equations.
5. These devices prepared the way for **electronic computers**.

### Conclusion

Teacher highlights that electro-mechanical devices were important steps toward today’s digital computers.

### Student Lesson Notes

- Electro-mechanical devices combine **electric power** with **moving parts** to do calculations.
- Examples include the **Jacquard loom**, **Hollerith tabulator**, and **differential analyzer**.
- They performed tasks faster and more accurately than earlier mechanical devices.
- Their punched-card control inspired later **programming methods**.

- **Entry Behaviour:** Students can count in base ten and have seen binary digits during introductory lessons.

  1. Define a number system and identify bases used in computing.
  2. Distinguish between decimal, binary, octal, and hexadecimal number systems.
  3. Convert numbers from decimal to binary, octal, and hexadecimal.
  4. Convert numbers from binary to decimal using place values.
  5. Solve simple word problems that require base conversions.

### Lesson Presentation

**Step 1 – Introduction:** Teacher displays digits 0–9 and letters A–F and asks students why computers use different symbols for counting.

**Step 2 – Presentation:** Teacher defines a number system as a method of representing numbers using a base and symbols.

**Step 3 – Explanation:** Teacher explains the bases 2, 8, 10, and 16, showing place values and how to read numbers in each system. Teacher demonstrates conversions step by step.

**Step 4 – Application:** Students work in pairs to complete conversion worksheets, converting between decimal, binary, octal, and hexadecimal numbers.

### Content Summary

2. Common number systems include **decimal (base 10)**, **binary (base 2)**, **octal (base 8)**, and **hexadecimal (base 16)**.
3. Decimal-to-binary conversion uses repeated division by 2; binary-to-decimal conversion multiplies digits by place values and adds results.
4. Octal and hexadecimal conversions can be done by grouping binary digits in threes or fours.
5. Understanding number systems helps in **programming**, **data representation**, and **computer troubleshooting**.

### Conclusion

Teacher reminds students that digital devices rely on binary, so learning number systems builds strong computing foundations.

### Student Lesson Notes

- Number systems have different **bases** and **symbols** (e.g., binary uses digits 0 and 1).
- Converting between bases involves **division**, **multiplication**, and **grouping binary digits**.
- Hexadecimal digits A–F represent decimal numbers **10–15**.
- Mastering conversions supports skills in **coding** and **computer hardware**.
